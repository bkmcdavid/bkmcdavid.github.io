The Problem & The Vision

We learn by doing—
and what we become
is what we do

"Legislators make citizens good by forming habits in them." — Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics 2.1

The public sphere is increasingly polarized and ideological. Among students, we observe two troubling responses: some readily jump on a party bandwagon, letting emotion cloud reason, while others—trepidatious of charged debate—choose apathy and disengagement.

The Habits of Discourse militates against both tendencies. Rooted in the Aristotelian insight that effective civil discourse requires ongoing civic practice, the project develops intellectual habits in students that encourage open and civil engagement. Through our "Dangerous Ideas" courses—targeting first-year students—and a series of public events aimed at the broader community, we cultivate the virtues that underlie productive disagreement.

Modern neuroscience characterizes the relationship between habit and repetition in terms that echo Aristotle: repeated practice shapes neural pathways, and these ingrained patterns predispose us to default to familiar modes of engagement. What constitutes our routines of civic engagement makes a profound difference to what we can enjoy, tolerate, or scarcely endure in our fellow citizens.

The Courses

Dangerous Ideas

First-year seminars that confront deeply held values through structured routines of argument, evidence, and debate—rewiring the way students approach disagreement.

◯

Openness

Curated reading lists induce a sense of the mildly overwhelming complexity of each issue. Just as Socrates induced perplexity, this tactic dissolves overconfidence and engenders the humility that arises in confrontation with what is complicated and surprising.

△

Humility

Students practice argument visualization, reproduce reasoning in mapping software, and critically evaluate data—learning to distinguish correlation from causation, selection bias from signal. The routine makes careful thinking habitual.

⬡

Courage

Oxford-style debates and encounters with classroom visitors who espouse controversial positions provide controlled, choreographed disruptions—chances to practice sticking to arguments and evidence even when emotions are rising.

Principal Investigators

The team behind Dangerous Ideas

Lead PI
Brennan McDavid
Assistant Professor of Philosophy · Chapman University

Brennan McDavid is a philosopher specializing in ancient theories of human nature and moral development. She earned her doctorate from Princeton's Program in Classical Philosophy and brings scholarly expertise and classroom experience with the theories of learning espoused by Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. Her published work spans ancient Greek ethics and political thought, with recent publications in Classical Quarterly, Ancient Philosophy, and the Journal of the History of Philosophy.

A member of Chapman's Smith Institute for Political Economy and Philosophy, McDavid has spearheaded the creation of an informal civil discourse society for Chapman's faculty and is eager to develop more civics programming for students from all disciplines.

Co-PI
Erik Kimbrough
Professor of Economics · Chapman University

Erik Kimbrough is an economist specializing in the study of norms and polarization. He has been PI or co-investigator on grant projects totaling over $4 million, including a $2.5M John Templeton Foundation grant on liberalism and a SSHRC Insight Grant on norms-based interventions to support democracy. His work has appeared in the American Economic Review, Journal of the European Economic Association, and the Journal of Political Economy.

His experiences co-teaching with McDavid and other Smith Institute faculty, merged with his research on norms and polarization, have informed the formatting and content of Dangerous Ideas courses since the pilot in Fall 2024.

Pilot data from Fall 2024 is encouraging. Students who completed the Dangerous Ideas course scored higher on openness and humility, and were less likely to support deplatforming of controversial speakers, compared to the average Chapman student and the average American college student—as measured by the FIRE College Free Speech Survey.

Over the three-year grant period, The Habits of Discourse aims to make Dangerous Ideas a fixture on Chapman's campus, building a constituency of professors passionate about and initiated to the pedagogical vision—ready to deploy their own iterations or integrate the methods into their existing courses.
