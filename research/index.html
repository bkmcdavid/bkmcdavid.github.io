Ancient Political Economy
Exploring the economic institutions, trade, and resource management of ancient societies, focusing on how political structures shaped and were shaped by economic life. Read more
My work draws on interdisciplinary methods—from textual analysis of primary sources to comparative studies—to illuminate how ancient economies functioned at both local and imperial levels. This research also considers the relevance of ancient thought for contemporary debates in political economy.
Plato's Theory of Human Nature
The education that Plato describes in the Republic is a story of nurture. My research in this area explores how Plato conceives of nurture as complementing inborn nature. Read more
My studies of Plato's conception of human nature begin from the premise, set out at 581c in the Republic, that there are "three primary kinds of people." This principle of Plato's moral psychology has implications for every dimension of the dialogue. Several of my papers explore these distinct dimensions:
  • “Prison-Breaking from Plato’s Cave.” In: Classical Quarterly (Forthcoming).
  • "The Psychology of Money in Plato’s Republic.” In: History of Political Economy (Forthcoming)
  • “Φύσιs in Plato’s Republic.” In: Ancient Philosophy 44 (2024), pp. 1–16
Adam Smith's Eudaimonism
Unpacking how Adam Smith’s ethics, economics, and aesthetics contribute to a philosophical framework for human flourishing—a modern ‘eudaimonism’. Read more
By tracing Smith’s engagement with ancient philosophy and his conceptions of sympathy, virtue, and the impartial spectator, I develop new interpretations of Smith’s relevance for current debates in moral philosophy and political theory.
Trust in AI
Examining the philosophical, ethical, and social conditions under which it is rational or justifiable to trust artificial intelligence systems. Read more
I address questions about explainability, moral agency, and the boundaries of human-machine collaboration, drawing on classical theories of trust and contemporary examples. My aim is to clarify the stakes of trust in an increasingly automated society.
